⚠ Safety T: Complete Guide

What is the Safety T?

The Safety T is the FDC's primary tool for verifying every fire mission falls within established safety limits before sending data to the guns. It is constructed from the Safety Card produced during the safety computation process and represents the boundaries of safe firing for a given PAA and target area.

If any element of the firing solution falls outside the limits, the mission is UNSAFE and you call CHECK FIRING.

Anatomy of the Safety T

                  +-----------+
                  |  MAX QE   |   Maximum Quadrant Elevation
                  +-----+-----+
                  |     |     |
              +---+ DEF | DEF +---+   Left/Right Deflection limits
              |   |     |     |   |
              +---+-----+-----+---+
                  |  MIN QE   |   Minimum Quadrant Elevation
                  +-----------+
                  |  MIN TI   |   Min Time fuze setting
                  +-----------+
                  |  MIN VT   |   Min VT fuze setting
                  +-----------+

8-Step Verification Process

  1. Verify SHELL/LOT against authorized list
  2. Verify CHARGE matches card
  3. Verify FUZE is authorized and compatible
  4. Check DEFLECTION between RIGHT DEF and LEFT DEF
  5. Check QE between MIN QE and MAX QE
  6. Check TI (if FZ TI) ≥ MIN TI
  7. Check VT (if FZ VT) ≥ MIN VT
  8. Final call: all pass = SAFE; any fail = UNSAFE

LARS Rule

Left Add, Right Subtract: deflection increases moving LEFT, decreases moving RIGHT. So LEFT DEF is the larger number, RIGHT DEF is the smaller. Valid DF falls BETWEEN these two values.

Section 1: Fundamentals (Q1-8)

Q1. List the five requirements for accurate fire.

  1. Accurate Target Location and Size
  2. Accurate Firing Unit Location
  3. Accurate Weapon and Ammunition Information
  4. Accurate Meteorological Information
  5. Accurate Computational Procedures

Q2. If the azimuth from point A to point B is 1400, what is the back azimuth?

Answer: c) 4600 mils

If forward AZ < 3200, ADD 3200. 1400 + 3200 = 4600.

Q3. An eight-digit grid coordinate gives a location to the nearest 10 meters.

Answer: a) True

4-digit = 1000m, 6-digit = 100m, 8-digit = 10m, 10-digit = 1m.

Q4. When plotting your platoon on a firing chart, plotting accuracy is ___ meter(s).

Answer: 10 meters

Q5. The common deflection for the M109-series howitzer is ___ mils.

Answer: b) 3200 mils

Reference deflection for laying the M109. Center of the deflection scale.

Q6. Define Quadrant Elevation (QE).

The angle between the base of the trajectory and the line of elevation, measured from the origin. Algebraic sum of angle of site and angle of elevation.

QE = Site + Elevation

Q7. What scale is used on the surveyed firing chart?

Answer: a) 1:25,000

Q8. When a target grid plots out of the safety box, what command from the chart operator?

Answer: b) Check firing

CHECK FIRING halts firing for safety. CEASE FIRING is administrative (mission complete).

Section 2: Definitions (Q9-17)

Q9. Define Maximum Ordinate (MAXORD).

The difference in altitude between the origin (gun) and the summit (highest point) of the trajectory.

Q10. Four components of the MTO, in order?

Answer: b) Unit to Fire, Changes/Additions to CFF, Number of Rounds, TGT #

UCNT

Q11. Define Technical Fire Direction.

The process of converting weapon and ammunition characteristics, weapon and target locations, and met info into firing data. Firing data: shell, charge, fuze, fuze setting, deflection, QE.

Q12. Define Tactical Fire Direction.

Processing calls for fire and determining appropriate method of fire, ammunition expenditure, units to fire, and time of attack.

Q13. FU ALT 732, TGT ALT 563. What is VI?

Answer: -169

VI = TGT ALT − FU ALT = 563 − 732 = −169

Negative = target below FU.

Q14. LARS stands for?

Left Add, Right Subtract

Applies to deflection corrections.

Q15. 4 types of ballistics?

  1. Interior: primer ignition to muzzle exit
  2. Transitional: muzzle exit until gases stop acting
  3. Exterior: flight through atmosphere
  4. Terminal: impact and after

Q16. Who can call CHECK FIRING?

Answer: d) Anyone who witnesses an unsafe act

Safety overrides rank.

Q17. Standard square weight of 155mm M107 (HE)?

Answer: c) 4sq or 95.0 lbs

📘 Extra: Manual Gunnery & Computations (15 Q)

Q61. What does GFT stand for?

Graphical Firing Table. A manual gunnery slide-rule tool for computing firing data without a TFT.

Q62. What are the 3 GFT scales?

Range, Elevation, Time of Flight (and Fuze Setting on the time-fuze GFT).

Q63. What is a GFT setting?

A registration correction applied to the GFT to account for non-standard conditions, derived from a registration mission. Includes a total range correction and total deflection correction.

Q64. What is the purpose of a registration mission?

To determine corrections for non-standard conditions by firing at a known point and computing the difference between actual and predicted impact.

Q65. What is the standard temperature?

59°F / 15°C / 288°K at the surface.

Q66. What is the standard air density (per TC 3-09.81)?

100% of standard reference (the ICAO standard atmosphere). All density variations from MET messages are expressed as a percentage of this baseline.

Q67. What is range probable error?

The distance from MPI within which 50% of rounds will fall in range. Found in TFT Table F.

Q68. What is deflection probable error?

The lateral distance from MPI within which 50% of rounds will fall.

Q69. What is the formula for total range correction?

Total Range K = (Achieved Range − Chart Range) / 1000 × 1000

Expressed in meters per 1000 meters of range.

Q70. What is "concurrent met"?

A met message taken at or near the time of registration, used to remove met effects from the registration corrections, leaving only position and velocity errors.

Q71. What are the 3 methods of registration?

Precision Registration, High-Burst Registration, Mean Point of Impact (MPI) Registration.

Q72. What is the angle of fall?

The acute angle between the descending branch of the trajectory and the horizontal plane at the level point.

Q73. What is the line of fall?

The tangent line at the level point on the descending branch of the trajectory.

Q74. What is "line of elevation"?

The line tangent to the trajectory at the origin (the line the tube is pointed).

Q75. What is drift?

The lateral deviation of the projectile from the plane of departure, caused by spin and gyroscopic forces. Always to the right for right-hand rifling.

🌤 Extra: MET & Atmospheric Effects (12 Q)

Q76. What is a MET message?

A coded report of atmospheric conditions (wind direction/speed, temperature, density/pressure) by altitude line, used to correct firing data for non-standard atmosphere.

Q77. How long is a computer MET message valid?

2 hours from the time of observation.

Q78. What is a MET Check Gauge?

A check on the existing GFT setting; firing one round and comparing the achieved impact vs predicted to determine if the setting is still good or needs update.

Q79. What is "met to a met"?

A technique for updating firing data when a new met arrives, by computing the difference between the new met and the old met used during registration, applied as a correction.

Q80. What is range wind?

The component of wind acting along the gun-target line, either head wind (decreases range) or tail wind (increases range).

Q81. What is cross wind?

The wind component perpendicular to the gun-target line, deflecting the round laterally.

Q82. How does temperature affect range?

Higher temperature = less dense air = less drag = longer range. Lower temperature = denser air = more drag = shorter range. Also affects propellant burn rate (hotter charge = higher MV).

Q83. What is air density expressed as in a MET?

Percentage of standard. Values below 100 indicate less dense air, above 100 indicate denser air.

Q84. What are the MET message types?

Computer MET (electronic, used by AFATDS), Ballistic MET (manual, used with GFT/TFT), Target Acquisition MET, Sound MET.

Q85. How is wind direction expressed in a MET?

In mils, measured clockwise from grid north. Direction is where the wind is coming FROM.

Q86. What is the MET line "00"?

The surface line, representing conditions at the firing unit's altitude.

Q87. How is wind speed expressed in a MET?

In knots.

📏 Extra: Site & VI Computations (10 Q)

Q88. What is angle of site?

The vertical angle between the horizontal plane at the gun and the line connecting gun and target. Positive when target is above gun, negative when below.

Q89. What is complementary angle of site?

A correction added to the angle of site to account for the non-rigidity of the trajectory at higher angles. Found in TFT Table G.

Q90. Site formula?

Site = Angle of Site + Complementary Angle of Site

Q91. Formula for angle of site (mils)?

Angle of Site = (VI / Range) × 1.0186 × 1000

VI in meters, Range in meters. Result in mils.

Q92. FU ALT 450, TGT ALT 680, find VI?

VI = +230

680 − 450 = +230 (target above FU)

Q93. FU ALT 1200, TGT ALT 1145, find VI?

VI = −55

1145 − 1200 = −55 (target below FU)

Q94. What is the level point?

The point on the descending branch where the projectile crosses the same altitude as the gun (origin level).

Q95. What is origin?

The center of the muzzle at the instant the projectile leaves the tube.

Q96. What does "ascending branch" refer to?

The portion of the trajectory from origin to summit (rising).

Q97. What does "descending branch" refer to?

The portion of the trajectory from summit to impact (falling).

📡 Extra: Call for Fire & Fire Mission (15 Q)

Q98. 6 elements of a Call for Fire?

  1. Observer Identification
  2. Warning Order
  3. Target Location
  4. Target Description
  5. Method of Engagement
  6. Method of Fire and Control

Q99. 3 methods of target location?

Grid, Polar, Shift from a Known Point.

Q100. What are the 3 types of fire missions?

Adjust Fire, Fire for Effect (FFE), Suppression.

Q101. What does FFE stand for?

Fire For Effect. Used when target location is accurate enough to engage without adjustment.

Q102. What does FPF stand for?

Final Protective Fire. A pre-planned barrier of fire to break up enemy assault. Called by maneuver unit on order.

Q103. Danger close range for 155mm?

600 meters or less from friendly troops to target.

Q104. Danger close range for 105mm?

600 meters or less.

Q105. Danger close for mortar 81mm/120mm?

81mm: 400m. 120mm: 600m.

Q106. Danger close for naval gunfire 5"?

750 meters or less.

Q107. What is bracketing in adjust fire?

Adjusting rounds to obtain one over and one short of the target, then splitting the bracket and going FFE.

Q108. What is creeping fire?

Adjusting rounds in 100m increments toward the target from a known safe distance. Used in danger close.

Q109. What does SPLASH mean?

Announced 5 seconds before impact so observer knows when to look for the round.

Q110. What does "Shot, over" mean?

The FDC's announcement to the observer that the rounds have left the tube.

Q111. What is Time on Target (TOT)?

A method of fire where multiple units coordinate so all rounds impact the target simultaneously.

Q112. What does "At My Command" mean?

The guns will fire only when the observer gives the command to fire, not immediately upon completion of fire commands.

💣 Extra: Ammunition, Fuzes & Propellants (15 Q)

Q113. What does RAP stand for?

Rocket-Assisted Projectile. M549 extends range using a rocket motor in the base.

Q114. What does DPICM stand for?

Dual-Purpose Improved Conventional Munitions. Submunition projectile (anti-personnel and anti-materiel).

Q115. What is the M119A2 charge system?

MACS (Modular Artillery Charge System). Used in 155mm, charges 1H through 5H low zone, and Charge 3 to 5 high zone.

Q116. What does MACS stand for?

Modular Artillery Charge System.

Q117. What is the M232A1?

MACS top zone charge module.

Q118. What is the M231?

MACS low zone charge module (charges 1-2).

Q119. What fuze does the M982 Excalibur use?

Integrated multi-option fuze (point detonating, delay, height of burst). Set via fuze setter / PIC interface.

Q120. What does the M1156 PGK do?

Precision Guidance Kit. Replaces standard fuze, uses GPS-guided spoilers to correct course to GPS-derived target. Tightens CEP from ~250m to under 30m.

Q121. What does M782 fuze do?

MOFA: Multi-Option Fuze Artillery. Replaces older fuzes, settable to Q, D, TI, or PROX.

Q122. What is FZ Q?

Quick (Point Detonating). Detonates on impact.

Q123. What is FZ D?

Delay. Detonates after a brief delay following impact (for penetration).

Q124. What is FZ TI?

Time. Detonates at a set time after firing, producing an airburst.

Q125. What is FZ VT?

Variable Time (Proximity). Detonates at a set HOB above target using radio proximity sensor.

Q126. Optimum height of burst for HE airburst?

20 meters above target for maximum fragmentation pattern against personnel in the open.

Q127. What shell type for illumination?

M485A2 ILLUM. Uses MTSQ or MT fuze for parachute deployment of illumination flare.

🛡 Extra: Safety & SDZ (12 Q)

Q128. What does SDZ stand for?

Surface Danger Zone. The ground/airspace designated for containment of munitions effects.

Q129. What are the components of an SDZ?

Area A (target impact area), Area B (dispersion area), Area C (ricochet area), Area D (vertical/aerial), Area E (lateral). Plus weapon danger zone and minimum safe distance.

Q130. What is a Risk Estimate Distance (RED)?

The minimum distance from target where friendly troops have a stated probability of incapacitation (0.1% PI is standard for combat).

Q131. What command halts firing immediately for safety?

CHECK FIRING. Different from CEASE FIRING (administrative end of mission).

Q132. When does FDC apply Special Munitions Safety?

When firing ICM, FASCAM, RAP, Copperhead, Excalibur, or other special munitions; each has unique safety constraints in its own safety T.

Q133. What is a Hot Tube?

A tube heated from sustained firing. Heat increases muzzle velocity and changes ballistic performance, requiring corrections.

Q134. What is a Cold Tube?

An unfired tube at or near ambient temperature. First-round corrections often differ from subsequent rounds.

Q135. Who develops the Safety T?

The Safety Officer / FDO, computed using the safety diagram and approved by the commander before live fire.

Q136. What is "left safety limit" and "right safety limit"?

The leftmost and rightmost azimuths from the PAA within which fire is authorized.

Q137. What is the minimum number of safety checks per fire mission?

Two independent computations (primary and check). Both must match before firing data is sent.

Q138. What's the difference between Safety Diagram and Safety T?

Diagram is the full graphical representation of the safety box. Safety T is the summary card extracted from the diagram for fast FDC verification.

Q139. What is loss of communications (LOSCOM) procedure?

Pre-established procedure for what to do when comms drop mid-mission. Generally: stop firing, attempt restoration, default to safe configuration.

📈 Extra: High Angle Fire (8 Q)

Q140. What QE range defines High Angle Fire?

Greater than 800 mils. Low angle is 0-800 mils.

Q141. When is high angle fire used?

To clear intervening crests, attack targets in defilade, attack targets close to the firing unit, or when low-angle clearance is impossible.

Q142. Disadvantage of high angle fire?

Longer time of flight, greater MET sensitivity, larger probable error, longer service time at the gun, increased radar signature.

Q143. Advantage of high angle fire?

Can reach behind obstacles, into deep defilade, and attack targets the gun cannot directly see.

Q144. What is the trade-off between high angle and low angle?

Low angle: faster, more accurate, less MET sensitive, but blocked by terrain. High angle: clears terrain, but slower, less accurate, more MET dependent.

Q145. Is angle of fall steeper in high angle?

Yes. High angle = steeper angle of fall = better for hitting targets in deep cover.

Q146. Are TFT tables for high and low angle the same?

No. Separate tables exist; high-angle TFT entries use different charge/elevation combinations.

Q147. Is the Safety T different for high angle vs low angle?

Yes. Separate Safety T required for each angle type, as QE limits and other parameters differ.

⚙ Extra: Battery Ops, Survey & Lay (8 Q)

Q148. What does PAA stand for?

Position Area for Artillery. The general area within which a firing unit emplaces.

Q149. What does PADS stand for?

Position Azimuth Determining System. Vehicle-mounted inertial nav for survey/lay.

Q150. What does IPADS stand for?

Improved Position Azimuth Determining System. Replaced PADS as the standard survey system.

Q151. What are the methods of laying a battery?

Reciprocal lay, simultaneous lay, orienting line lay, hasty lay, M2 aiming circle methods.

Q152. What is the M2 aiming circle used for?

To lay the battery on the azimuth of fire and to determine the initial common deflection.

Q153. What does RSOP stand for?

Reconnaissance, Selection, and Occupation of Position.

Q154. What are the phases of occupation?

Reconnaissance, advance party movement, selection, marking, occupation, lay, ready to fire.

Q155. What is the minimum spacing between howitzers?

Standard is 100 meters between howitzers. Adjusted based on threat and terrain.

📻 Extra: AFATDS & Communications (5 Q)

Q156. What does AFATDS stand for?

Advanced Field Artillery Tactical Data System. Primary digital FDC system.

Q157. What does FBCB2 stand for?

Force XXI Battle Command Brigade and Below. Replaced by JCR.

Q158. What does JCR stand for?

Joint Capabilities Release. Mounted situational awareness system, successor to FBCB2.

Q159. What is COMSEC?

Communications Security. Crypto keys, codes, and equipment used to secure radio traffic. Managed by the FDC and section.

Q160. What is the SKL?

Simple Key Loader. Handheld device for transferring crypto keys to radios and other comms equipment.
